On August 26, 2013 at 4pm, a group of organizations and individuals, including Restore the Fourth SF, representing the broad StopWatching.Us coalition met with House Minority Leader Nancy Pelosi's chief of staff in San Francisco, Dan Bernal.

Coalition representing StopWatching.Us

  • Tracy Rosenberg, Media Alliance
  • Jennifer Granick, Stanford Law
  • Mark Jaycox, EFF
  • Anna Salem, ACLU
  • Matt Lockshin, Credo Action
  • Jonathan, Hackers and Founders
  • Nadia Kayyali, Bill of Rights Defense Committee
  • Michael Poon, Restore the Fourth SF

Over the course of 45 minutes, the coalition members covered the following points:

  • We want an end to Section 215 phone metadata collection, an end to Section 702 internet data collection, and transparency, including a release of secret court opinions.
  • We want a complete change, not tweaks. Specifically…
  • We want bulk collection to be stopped right now. That may take away some valuable capabilities/it may be a blunt thing, but it's gone so far wrong that it needs to stop right now — especially since we don't know the full scope of what's been going wrong. Specific capabilities can possibly be reinstated after, but bulk collection as it exists now must stop immediately.
  • Bulk collection and our principles/privacy/checks and balances are fundamentally at odds. The FISA Court doesn't work.
  • We need to hear a clear statement of principles from Pelosi on the matter that we can hold her to.
  • The solution to the surveillance overreach will likely come in several bills, since most likely, no one bill will nix all the concerns.
  • The surveillance hurts SF economics.
  • Pelosi needs to hold town halls and speak to constituents. Tele-town halls are okay.
  • Briefly: A mention of better whistleblower protections.
  • Briefly: The traditional FISA (before FISA Amendments Act of 2008) was actually better for privacy.

While we were there, I also delivered the public comments on the NSA scandal that we gathered at our August 4th event (pictured above).

Pelosi's office responded with the following:

  • They are a foreign policy office, not a legislative office, so they cannot comment on specifics.
  • Pelosi wants action in the fall. She will not wait for bill reauthorization (2015).
  • Pelosi/her office has not stonewalled us or anyone else on this issue.
  • Pelosi voted against the Amash Amendment because it was too blunt of an instrument. It was the wrong vehicle for change.
  • Standard fare: Pelosi has always had a strong position on privacy. The FISA Amendments Act of 2008 did many good things for privacy. Et cetera.

We, of course, were not able to extract any specific promises, but we clearly and strongly communicated what was necessary to fix this mess.

One coalition attendee, Tracy Rosenberg from Media Alliance, described it like this

The quickest way to describe the 45-minute meeting is as a tug-of-war between two ideas:

a) blanket surveillance is fundamentally unconstitutional and needs to stop

and

b) NSA surveillance programs need to be tweaked to rein in some level of over-reach.

Pelosi's office has agreed to meet with the coalition again when bills have moved further along the legislative cycle. They also gave us a summary of the public statements Pelosi's made, including an open letter to Obama.
