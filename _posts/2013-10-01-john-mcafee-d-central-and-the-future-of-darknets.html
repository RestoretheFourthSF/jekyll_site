Today, John McAfee announced a forthcoming hardware product called D-Central to provide consumers with private networking services that would be resistant to NSA scrutiny. He has described his product in only the highest level terms but we can infer a lot from his description and the fundamental architecture of the Internet.

The D-Central seems to incorporate ideas similar to that of the MeshBox or the PirateBox.  What all these initiatives have in common is they create a public environment where users can communicate privately without disclosing either content or metadata to the Internet providers. Most other encryption methods only prevent disclosure of  content.

 

I hope that D-Central, Meshbox and others can offer an accessible consumer product that can provide a mass surveillance resistant Internet experience to the masses. This is clearly possible: VPN providers, botnets, et cetera all operate darknets that have been highly resistant to compromise and surveillance.

 

For this kind of privacy-friendly Internet to truly succeed, it needs to provide both a developer platform and a business model. The centralized NSA friendly Internet developed because of the seductive appeal of easy applications development in data centers and the success of the advertising model. Without a developer platform, these experiments in Darknets are only going to be able to deliver a Web 1.0 experience of messaging and file sharing rather than compelling applications.

 

There are potentially compelling aspects to an NSA-proof internet for developers. They would have to raise less capital for bandwidth and servers because costs would be distributed among their users. That's a business model, but is it enough to drive development?
